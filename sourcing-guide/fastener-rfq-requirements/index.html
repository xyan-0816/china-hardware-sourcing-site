Fastener RFQ and QC Documents Guide

A useful fastener RFQ should define product, standard, size, grade, finish, quantity, packing, application, and required inspection documents before price discussion. Clear RFQ data reduces sourcing risk before production and shipment review.

Quick Buyer Conclusion

A useful fastener RFQ should define product, standard, size, grade, finish, quantity, packing, application, and required inspection documents before price discussion. If these points are missing, the quotation may look complete but still hide technical, packing, or document risk.

Fastener RFQ review should happen before formal quotation because bolts, nuts, washers, rods, screws, and anchors can look similar in photos while requiring different standards, thread forms, grades, finishes, and inspection records.

What a Clear RFQ Prevents

A clear RFQ prevents wrong standard interpretation, wrong thread pitch, mismatched bolt and nut classes, unsuitable surface finish, missing document expectations, and packing data delays. It also helps the supplier ask technical questions before production instead of discovering problems during final inspection or shipment preparation.

Buyers should confirm final requirements based on drawings, BOM, project specifications, samples, or purchase lists.

Fastener RFQ Checklist

  • Product name
  • Standard
  • Drawing or sample
  • Diameter
  • Length
  • Thread pitch
  • Full thread / partial thread
  • Grade / property class
  • Material
  • Surface finish
  • Quantity
  • Packing
  • Destination
  • Inspection documents
  • Matching nuts or washers if required
  • Application environment

Common Mistakes and Risk Notes

  • Requesting price before defining grade and finish
  • Using photos instead of drawings for critical dimensions
  • Forgetting thread pitch
  • Not specifying full thread or partial thread
  • Not matching nuts and washers
  • Asking for documents after production is finished
  • Ignoring packing and carton data
  • Accepting standard substitutions without written buyer confirmation

Lot Traceability Chain

A practical traceability chain is: carton -> inspection lot -> production batch -> heat number -> raw material certificate. This chain helps the buyer connect packed goods to inspection records and material documents when project requirements call for traceability.

Traceability should be discussed before shipment. If cartons are mixed without clear lot relation, later document review can become difficult even when individual reports exist.

Example Wording for Sourcing Communication

Example wording: “Supplier should provide inspection records matching the agreed standard, size, grade, finish, and production lot before shipment.”

Example wording: “Any standard substitution should be confirmed by buyer before production.” These examples are sourcing communication references only. Buyers should review final contract wording with their own quality, engineering, or legal team where needed.

Document Review Before Quotation

Before formal quotation, buyers should decide which documents are required and which are optional. A general hardware order may need fewer documents than a controlled project order. A first container, Grade 8.8 bolt program, surface-treated item, or mixed-SKU shipment may need more document planning because product, packing, and traceability risk are higher.

The RFQ should also define whether third-party inspection is required, whether pre-shipment photos are enough for visual packing review, and whether the supplier must provide carton and pallet data before loading.

FAQ

Practical answers for overseas buyers preparing fastener sourcing documents.

What information should be included in a fastener RFQ?

A useful RFQ should include product name, standard, drawing or sample, diameter, length, thread pitch, thread type, grade, material, finish, quantity, packing, destination, and required documents.

What QC documents should buyers request before shipment?

Depending on the order, buyers may request MTC/MTR, certificate of conformity, dimensional report, hardness report, tensile or proof-load report, thread gauge report, coating report, packing inspection report, and photos.

What is the difference between MTC and MTR?

Both relate to material test documentation. The exact wording depends on supplier and project requirements, so buyers should define the required document type before production.

Why does thread GO / NO-GO inspection matter?

Thread gauges help confirm whether bolts, nuts, rods, or tapped parts fit the agreed thread requirement before shipment.

When should coating thickness or salt spray reports be requested?

They should be requested when finish performance or project documents require verification, and the requirement should be agreed before production.

How should buyers confirm lot traceability?

Buyers can ask how cartons connect to inspection lots, production batches, heat numbers, and raw material certificates.

Why should RFQ review happen before formal quotation?

Early RFQ review reduces missing specifications, unclear documents, wrong finish assumptions, and shipment packing surprises.
