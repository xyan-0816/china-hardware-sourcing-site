DIN and ISO Fastener Standards Comparison

Quick Buyer Conclusion

DIN 933 and ISO 4017 are commonly used for full-thread hex bolts, while DIN 931 and ISO 4014 are commonly used for partial-thread hex bolts. Buyers should not assume these standards are automatically interchangeable; final selection should follow drawings, BOM, project specifications, assembly requirements, or approved samples.

For procurement teams, the practical decision is not only the standard name. The buyer should confirm whether the assembly needs full thread or a smooth shank section, whether the nut and washer set matches the bolt, and whether the project specification accepts a DIN-to-ISO substitution. A drawing note or BOM line should normally override a general website description.

How Buyers Should Read These Standards

A standard reference controls more than the product name. It may affect dimensions, thread length, head dimensions, tolerance expectations, marking, property class, and how the supplier checks the part. When a purchase list only says “hex bolt DIN 933” or “ISO 4014 bolt,” the supplier still needs diameter, length, pitch, grade, finish, quantity, packaging, and any inspection document requirement.

Buyers should confirm final requirements based on drawings, BOM, project specifications, samples, or purchase lists.

Full Thread vs Partial Thread

Full-thread bolts may be useful when full-length adjustability or short clamping length is needed. They can give thread engagement across most of the bolt length, which may fit some equipment, brackets, and general hardware assemblies. However, full thread is not automatically better. Threaded sections have different contact behavior from a smooth shank, and the project specification may require a defined grip length.

Partial-thread bolts may be preferred when the unthreaded shank supports shear area, alignment, or bearing surface requirements. In some assemblies, the shank is intended to sit through the joint while the threaded portion engages the nut. Application engineers or buyer specifications should confirm final selection.

Procurement Mistakes to Avoid

  • Mixing DIN and ISO without confirming dimensions
  • Replacing partial thread with full thread without approval
  • Ignoring thread pitch when copying an old purchase list
  • Ignoring grade / property class on the RFQ
  • Mixing coating requirements between bolt, nut, and washer
  • Not checking nut and washer compatibility
  • Assuming the same length gives the same grip condition
  • Approving a substitute only from website names instead of drawings

RFQ Information Checklist

  • Required standard
  • Full thread or partial thread
  • Diameter
  • Length
  • Thread pitch
  • Property class
  • Material
  • Surface finish
  • Quantity
  • Matching nuts / washers
  • Drawings or samples
  • Application environment
  • Packing and label requirements
  • Required inspection documents

Document Examples Buyers Can Request

For a first order or project-controlled purchase, buyers may ask the supplier to show a dimensional inspection format, thread GO / NO-GO gauge record, coating thickness report where a finish is specified, and material certificate where the purchase requires traceability. These documents should match the actual standard, size, grade, and finish agreed in the RFQ.

If a supplier suggests changing DIN 931 to ISO 4014, or DIN 933 to ISO 4017, the buyer should ask what changes, what stays the same, and whether the drawing owner accepts the substitution. This protects the buyer from silent standard changes during quotation.

Buyer Review Workflow

Start with the drawing or BOM, identify whether the bolt is full thread or partial thread, confirm diameter and length, then confirm pitch, property class, material, finish, nuts, washers, and packaging. If the old purchase record uses a DIN reference but the new project uses ISO, record the reason for the change and keep the approval trail with the RFQ file.

For repeat orders, buyers should also compare the previous packing list, inspection documents, and product photos against the new RFQ. Small differences in thread length or finish can create assembly issues even when the product name looks similar.

FAQ

Practical answers for overseas buyers preparing fastener sourcing documents.

What is the difference between DIN 933 and ISO 4017?

Both are commonly used for full-thread hex bolts, but buyers should still verify dimensions, thread pitch, property class, coating, drawing notes, and project approval before using one as a substitute for the other.

What is the difference between DIN 931 and ISO 4014?

Both are commonly used for partial-thread hex bolts. The buyer should check shank length, thread length, diameter, length, tolerance expectations, and whether the project accepts the ISO or DIN reference.

Can DIN and ISO hex bolt standards be used interchangeably?

They should not be treated as automatically interchangeable. Drawings, BOM, project specifications, customer approval, and application requirements should decide final selection.

When should buyers choose full-thread hex bolts?

Full-thread hex bolts may be useful when adjustment along most of the bolt length is required, clamping length is short, or the drawing specifically calls for full thread.

When should buyers choose partial-thread hex bolts?

Partial-thread bolts may be preferred when an unthreaded shank is needed for shear area, alignment, bearing surface, or assembly behavior specified by engineering documents.

What information should be included in a DIN / ISO hex bolt RFQ?

Include standard, full or partial thread, diameter, length, thread pitch, property class, material, finish, quantity, packing, drawings or samples, and matching nut or washer requirements.

Should buyers confirm nut and washer compatibility with DIN / ISO bolts?

Yes. Nuts and washers should be checked for thread, grade or class, finish, dimensions, coating thickness, and application requirements.
