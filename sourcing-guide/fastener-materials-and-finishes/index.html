Fastener Surface Treatment Comparison

Fastener surface treatment should be selected by application environment, corrosion risk, thread fit, coating thickness, strength grade, and required verification documents. Zinc plating, hot-dip galvanizing, zinc flake coating, and stainless steel each solve different sourcing problems.

Quick Buyer Conclusion

Fastener surface treatment should be selected by application environment, corrosion risk, thread fit, coating thickness, strength grade, and required verification documents. Zinc plating, hot-dip galvanizing, zinc flake coating, and stainless steel each solve different sourcing problems.

Indoor general use often considers zinc plating. Outdoor construction or exposed assemblies may consider hot-dip galvanizing. High-strength or precision assemblies may require careful coating control. Coastal, humid, or chemical environments may require stainless steel 304 / 316 or engineering review. No finish should be described as absolutely best without the application context.

How Buyers Should Choose

Buyers should begin with exposure conditions, not appearance. The same silver or grey surface can represent different coating systems, thickness levels, and test expectations. A good RFQ should define whether the fastener is used indoors, outdoors, near coastal humidity, in machinery, near chemicals, or in a structural assembly.

Buyers should confirm final requirements based on drawings, BOM, project specifications, samples, or purchase lists.

Zinc Plating

Zinc plating is common for indoor or general machinery applications where corrosion exposure is controlled. Buyers should still define coating thickness, passivation color if relevant, salt spray expectations if required, and whether the fastener is high strength. For high-strength fasteners, hydrogen embrittlement relief may be relevant depending on grade, plating process, and buyer specification.

Buyers may request a coating thickness report or salt spray report when the project requires verification. They should avoid asking only for a visual finish, because appearance does not confirm coating thickness or process control.

Hot-Dip Galvanizing

Hot-dip galvanizing is often used for outdoor construction and exposed assemblies. The thicker coating can improve corrosion protection, but it may affect thread fit. Matching nuts should be confirmed because the nut thread may need suitable allowance or compatible processing.

Buyers should confirm HDG standard, coating thickness, assembly fit, nut matching, washer finish, and whether the product dimensions remain acceptable after coating. HDG should not be selected only because the part will be outdoors; project standards and fit requirements still control the final decision.

Zinc Flake Coating

Zinc flake coating is often considered for corrosion resistance with lower hydrogen embrittlement risk than electroplating in certain high-strength applications. It is not only a color choice. It requires supplier process control, coating specification, curing control, and test requirements that match the buyer's project.

Buyers should confirm whether the coating system, salt spray expectations, friction requirements if relevant, and dimensional checks are specified. When a supplier proposes zinc flake as an alternative to zinc plating or HDG, the buyer should ask what documents can verify the actual coating system.

Stainless Steel 304 / 316

Stainless steel 304 is often used for general corrosion resistance, while 316 / A4 may be considered for coastal, marine, chemical, or humid environments where appropriate. Buyers should not assume stainless steel equals high tensile strength. Stainless fasteners may have different property classes, cost consideration, and assembly behavior from carbon steel fasteners.

Galling risk may need consideration, especially in stainless bolt and nut assemblies. Buyers should confirm material grade, thread fit, lubrication or assembly guidance if needed, and whether the application requires magnetic, strength, or corrosion criteria beyond a simple material name.

Surface Finish RFQ Checklist

  • Application environment
  • Indoor / outdoor / coastal / chemical exposure
  • Required corrosion resistance
  • Fastener grade
  • Thread fit requirement
  • Coating thickness requirement
  • Salt spray requirement
  • Required standard
  • Matching nut and washer finish
  • Required documents
  • Hydrogen embrittlement relief if relevant
  • Packaging protection after coating

Common Mistakes

  • Choosing finish only by appearance
  • Mixing HDG bolts with zinc-plated nuts without checking fit
  • Ignoring hydrogen embrittlement risk for high-strength plated fasteners
  • Asking for salt spray hours without defining coating system
  • Assuming stainless steel is always stronger
  • Forgetting matching washers and nuts
  • Ignoring packaging protection for coated parts
  • Approving a finish without document expectations

Document Review Before Shipment

For surface-treated fasteners, documents should match the agreed finish and actual production lot. A coating thickness report is useful when thickness is specified. A salt spray report may be useful when the buyer has a defined coating system and test expectation. For stainless steel, a material certificate can help confirm grade. For high-strength plated fasteners, a buyer may ask whether hydrogen embrittlement relief applies and what process record can support it.

FAQ

Practical answers for overseas buyers preparing fastener sourcing documents.

What is the difference between zinc plating and hot-dip galvanizing?

Zinc plating is often thinner and used for indoor or general applications. Hot-dip galvanizing is thicker and often considered for outdoor construction, but thread fit and matching nuts must be checked.

When should buyers choose zinc flake coating?

Zinc flake coating may be considered for corrosion resistance with careful coating control, especially where high-strength fasteners require a process review.

Is stainless steel always better than coated carbon steel?

No. Stainless steel is not automatically better than coated carbon steel; corrosion exposure, strength, cost, galling risk, magnetic requirements, and project specifications should be reviewed.

Why does coating thickness matter for fasteners?

Coating thickness affects corrosion performance, thread fit, gauge results, assembly feel, and whether matching nuts or washers need a compatible finish.

What is hydrogen embrittlement relief?

For some high-strength electroplated fasteners, baking or relief control may be required to reduce hydrogen embrittlement risk. Buyers should confirm whether it applies to their grade and process.

Should nuts and washers use the same finish as bolts?

Often they should be compatible, but the exact requirement depends on the assembly, coating thickness, thread fit, corrosion plan, and buyer specification.

What documents can verify surface treatment quality?

Useful documents may include coating thickness reports, salt spray reports where required, process references, material certificates, and packing inspection records.
