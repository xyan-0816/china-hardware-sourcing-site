Bolt Grades and Property Classes Guide

Metric property classes, SAE grades and ASTM grades belong to different fastener classification systems and must not be treated as automatically equivalent. Buyers should confirm the applicable standard, product dimensions, material, mechanical requirements and project specification before selecting or substituting a fastener grade. Prices, MOQ, packing, documentation and lead time are confirmed after manual RFQ review.

What Is a Metric Bolt Property Class?

A metric bolt property class is a designation defined by ISO standards that indicates the mechanical properties of the bolt. The class is typically shown as two numbers separated by a dot, such as 4.6, 5.6, 6.8, 8.8, 10.9 or 12.9. The first number multiplied by 100 gives the nominal tensile strength in megapascals (MPa) as defined by the standard. The second number divided by 10 gives the yield strength ratio expressed as a decimal of the nominal tensile strength.

For example, property class 8.8 as defined by ISO 898-1 has a nominal tensile strength of approximately 800 MPa and a yield strength of approximately 640 MPa. These values are defined and tested per the standard. Actual tested values and tolerances depend on the product, size range, testing method, and supplier quality control. Buyers should confirm the applicable ISO standard, product dimensions, and required test methods before ordering.

How Property Classes 8.8, 10.9 and 12.9 Compare

The most commonly specified metric bolt property classes are 8.8, 10.9 and 12.9. These classes represent increasing nominal tensile strength ranges defined by ISO 898-1 for carbon steel and alloy steel bolts. Class 8.8 is commonly used in general industrial and automotive applications where moderate strength is sufficient. Class 10.9 is specified for higher-load metric bolted assemblies. Class 12.9 represents the highest commonly available strength level and requires careful attention to material, thread processing, hardness, and hydrogen embrittlement risk during finishing.

Each property class requires a matching nut class per the applicable standard. Class 8 bolts are typically used with 8.8 bolts, Class 10 nuts with 10.9 bolts, and Class 12 nuts with 12.9 bolts. Matching washers should also be reviewed. The property class alone does not guarantee performance in any specific application. Buyers should confirm the bolt standard, nut grade, washer requirements, and project specification before selecting a class.

What SAE Grades 2, 5 and 8 Mean

SAE J429 defines grades for inch-series bolts, cap screws, and studs. The most commonly specified grades are Grade 2, Grade 5 and Grade 8. Grade 2 is a low-strength grade for general-purpose inch fasteners. Grade 5 requires a minimum tensile strength of 120,000 psi for diameters up to 1 inch as defined by the standard. Grade 8 has a minimum tensile strength of 150,000 psi for diameters up to 1 inch per SAE J429.

Grade markings differ by grade. Grade 5 bolts typically have three radial lines on the head. Grade 8 bolts typically have six radial lines. Grade 2 bolts may not have radial line markings. Buyers should confirm the SAE J429 grade, thread series, fastener dimensions, nut grade, washer requirements, and applicable project specification before ordering inch-series fasteners. SAE grades are not directly equivalent to metric property classes.

What ASTM A307 and ASTM F3125 Grades Represent

ASTM A307 covers carbon steel bolts and studs with a specified minimum tensile strength of 60,000 psi for Grade A. This standard is used in general non-critical applications where higher strength grades are not required. ASTM A307 Grade B covers studs and threaded rods for flanged joints in piping systems. Buyers should confirm the ASTM grade, product type, size range, finish, and applicable code requirements before ordering.

ASTM F3125 is a comprehensive standard that covers high-strength structural bolts and replaces earlier standards including A325 and A490. F3125 includes multiple grades such as A325, A490, F1852 and F2280 for different structural applications. These bolts are used in structural steel connections where bolt dimensions, nut grade, washer type, installation method, and preload requirements are specified in the project specification. F3125 bolts are not interchangeable with property class or SAE grade bolts without reviewing the structural design and applicable building code.

Why Metric Classes and SAE/ASTM Grades Are Not Direct Equivalents

Metric property classes, SAE J429 grades and ASTM grades belong to separate classification systems developed under different standards organizations. The systems differ in thread form, dimensional standards, mechanical property testing methods, and quality verification requirements. A metric Class 8.8 bolt and an SAE Grade 5 bolt are not formally equivalent despite having similar nominal tensile strength ranges. Substitution should only be considered when the applicable standard, dimensions, thread, nut grade, washer requirement, and project specification have all been verified.

Buyers should never assume that a metric class and an SAE or ASTM grade are directly interchangeable. Each system references its own testing standard, material specification, and marking requirement. The final decision should be based on the applicable standard for the intended application, not on a comparison table that claims direct equivalence.

How Bolt Grades Should Be Matched with Nut Property Classes

Bolt and nut property classes should be matched according to the requirements of the applicable standard. Under ISO 898-2 for metric nuts, a Class 8 nut is typically matched with a Class 8.8 bolt, Class 10 with 10.9, and Class 12 with 12.9. Under SAE J995 for inch-series nuts, nut grades corresponding to SAE bolt grades should be selected. Under ASTM standards, the matching rule is specified in the applicable standard for the fastener assembly.

Matching nut class, thread fit, finish, coating type, and dimensional compatibility should all be confirmed with the supplier. Using a nut with a lower property class than recommended may affect the assembly performance. The buyer should specify both the bolt grade and the nut grade in the RFQ and confirm the standard reference for both components.

When Hardened Washers May Be Required

Hardened washers are typically specified when the applicable standard, project specification, or structural design requires them. In structural bolting per ASTM F3125, hardened washers are specified when required for bolt preload, joint design, or installation method. In metric high-strength applications, hardened washers may be specified per standards such as ISO 7089 or ISO 7090 with hardness requirements matched to the bolt property class.

The need for hardened washers depends on the joint design, bolt grade, nut type, and installation method. Buyers should confirm the washer standard, hardness range, dimensions, and finish with the supplier. The applicable standard and project specification take precedence over general guidance.

What Grade Markings Buyers Should Confirm

Grade markings on bolts are defined by the applicable standard and typically include the manufacturer identification mark and the property class or grade designation. For metric bolt property classes per ISO 898-1, the marking includes the property class number and the manufacturer identifier. For SAE J429 grades, Grade 5 bolts typically have three radial lines and Grade 8 bolts have six radial lines, along with the manufacturer mark. ASTM F3125 structural bolts have specific marking requirements defined in the standard, including grade designation and manufacturer identifier.

Buyers should confirm marking requirements with the supplier and request sample images or drawings before production. Markings should match the specified standard and grade. The absence of markings, inconsistent markings, or markings that do not match the ordered grade are flags that require clarification with the supplier.

What Grade Information Belongs in an RFQ

When preparing a grade-related RFQ, buyers should include the following information so the supplier can review each fastener against the specified standard:

  • Bolt grade property class or grade designation
  • Applicable standard, including standard number and year where specified
  • Thread system and dimensions
  • Material specification
  • Nut grade and standard
  • Washer requirements and standard
  • Finish and coating requirements
  • Marking requirements
  • Required inspection documents and test reports
  • Project specification or application context where relevant

Each item should be confirmed with the supplier before production. The RFQ should not assume that a grade designation alone defines all requirements for a fastener assembly.

Common Grade-Selection and Substitution Mistakes

The following are common mistakes that buyers should be aware of when selecting or substituting fastener grades:

  • Assuming metric property classes and SAE grades are direct equivalents based on nominal tensile strength values alone.
  • Substituting a different grade without confirming dimensional standards, thread fit, nut matching, and washer compatibility.
  • Mismatching nut and bolt grades by ordering a lower-strength nut than the bolt grade requires.
  • Selecting a higher-strength grade assuming it always improves assembly performance.
  • Requesting a substitution without reviewing the applicable project specification, building code, or design standard.
  • Using a single substituted grade where the specification requires a complete fastener assembly with matching nut, washer, and installation method.
  • Ordering fasteners by grade designation only without specifying the applicable standard.

Engineering or project requirements take precedence over general guidance. Grade selection and substitution should be confirmed by the buyer's engineering, quality, or project team.

FAQ

Practical answers for overseas buyers selecting or confirming fastener grades and property classes.

What is a metric bolt property class?

A metric bolt property class is a designation defined by ISO standards that indicates the mechanical properties of the bolt, typically shown as two numbers separated by a dot, such as 8.8, 10.9 or 12.9, and the numbers represent standard-defined tensile and yield strength ranges verified through specified testing methods.

What does property class 8.8 mean?

Property class 8.8 is a metric bolt designation defined by ISO 898-1, where the first number indicates a nominal tensile strength of approximately 800 MPa (rounded value per the standard) and the second number indicates a yield strength ratio of 0.8 of the nominal tensile strength, resulting in a minimum yield strength of approximately 640 MPa as defined by the standard.

What is the difference between 8.8 and 10.9 bolts?

The difference between property class 8.8 and 10.9 bolts is in their standard-defined mechanical property ranges, where 10.9 has a higher nominal tensile strength and a higher yield strength ratio of 0.9, and the two classes are not interchangeable without engineering review, fastener standard confirmation, and matching nut and washer coordination.

What is the difference between 10.9 and 12.9 bolts?

The difference between property class 10.9 and 12.9 bolts is that 12.9 represents a higher strength level defined by ISO 898-1, with a higher nominal tensile strength and a yield strength ratio of 0.9, and 12.9 bolts require careful attention to material, thread processing, hardness, and hydrogen embrittlement risk during finishing.

What does SAE Grade 5 mean?

SAE Grade 5 is a grade defined by SAE J429 for inch-series bolts, requiring a minimum tensile strength of 120,000 psi for diameters up to 1 inch as specified in the standard, and is commonly applied to inch-series hex bolts, cap screws, and related fasteners in general industrial and automotive applications.

What is the difference between SAE Grade 5 and Grade 8?

SAE Grade 8 bolts have higher mechanical property requirements than Grade 5 under SAE J429, with a specified minimum tensile strength of 150,000 psi for diameters up to 1 inch, and Grade 8 fasteners are typically used in higher-load inch-series applications where the applicable standard, assembly specification, and matching hardware are confirmed.

What does SAE Grade 2 mean?

SAE Grade 2 is a grade defined by SAE J429 for inch-series bolts with lower mechanical property requirements, commonly specified for low-strength general-purpose inch fasteners where the applicable standard does not require higher grades such as Grade 5 or Grade 8.

What is ASTM A307?

ASTM A307 is an ASTM standard covering carbon steel bolts and studs with a specified minimum tensile strength of 60,000 psi for Grade A, and it is commonly used in general non-critical applications where higher-strength grades such as SAE Grade 5 or Grade 8 are not required by the specification.

What is ASTM F3125?

ASTM F3125 is an ASTM standard that covers high-strength structural bolts and replaces earlier standards, covering Grades A325 and A490, and it is used in structural steel connections where the project specification, bolt dimensions, nut grade, and washer requirements are confirmed before installation.

Is Class 8.8 the same as SAE Grade 5?

Class 8.8 and SAE Grade 5 belong to different classification systems and are not formally equivalent, and buyers should confirm the applicable standard, thread system, dimensions, mechanical requirements, and project specification before considering any substitution.

Can metric and inch bolt grades be substituted?

Metric and inch bolt grades cannot be substituted based on grade designation alone because the two systems differ in thread form, dimensional standards, mechanical property testing methods, and nut and washer matching requirements, and any substitution should be reviewed by engineering against the project specification.

How should bolt and nut property classes be matched?

Bolt and nut property classes should be matched according to the applicable standard, which typically specifies the minimum nut class required for each bolt property class, such as using a Class 8 nut with an 8.8 bolt or a Class 10 nut with a 10.9 bolt, and the buyer should confirm the nut standard, thread fit, finish, and grade marking with the supplier.

When should hardened washers be used with high-strength bolts?

Hardened washers should be used with high-strength bolts when the applicable standard, project specification, or structural design requires them, and buyers should confirm the washer standard, hardness range, dimensions, and finish match with the bolt and nut requirements.

What grade markings are used on bolts?

Grade markings on bolts are defined by the applicable standard and typically include manufacturer identification, property class or grade designation, and additional marks such as radial lines on SAE Grade 5 and Grade 8 bolts, and buyers should confirm marking requirements and review samples before production.

What grade information should buyers include in an RFQ?

Buyers should include the bolt grade or property class, the applicable standard, thread system, diameter, length, material specification, finish, nut and washer grade requirements, marking requirements, and required inspection documents in the RFQ, and each of these items should be confirmed with the supplier before production.

What are common grade-selection and substitution mistakes?

Common grade-selection and substitution mistakes include treating metric property classes and SAE grades as direct equivalents, substituting grades without confirming dimensional standards and thread fit, mismatching nut and bolt grades, assuming higher-strength grades always improve safety, and substituting without reviewing the project specification and application requirements.

How do stainless steel property classes compare to carbon steel?

Stainless steel fastener property classes are defined by different standards such as ISO 3506 for metric sizes and follow a different designation system than carbon steel property classes, so stainless steel grades should not be compared or substituted with carbon steel grades based on designation alone without reviewing the applicable standard and project requirements.

What does property class 4.6, 5.6, 6.8 or 8.8 indicate?

Metric bolt property classes such as 4.6, 5.6, 6.8 and 8.8 are designated under ISO 898-1 where the first number multiplied by 100 indicates the nominal tensile strength in MPa and the second number divided by 10 indicates the yield strength ratio, and buyers should confirm the required class against the applicable standard, nut grade, and project specification.
