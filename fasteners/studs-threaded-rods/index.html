Stud Bolts, Double-End Studs and Threaded Rods

Studs and threaded rods from China are widely sourced for flange connections, pipe supports, structural anchoring, machinery assembly, and general industrial threaded fastening applications. Common types include fully threaded rods, stud bolts, double-end studs, tap-end studs, flange connection studs, anchor rods, and cut-to-length threaded rods. Buyers should confirm product type, standard or drawing, diameter, thread pitch or TPI, overall length, threaded length at each end, plain centre length where applicable, material and grade, surface finish, and matching nut requirements before quotation. Standard references may include DIN 976, ISO 898, ASTM A193, ASTM A307, ASTM F1554, or project-specific drawings. We support sourcing communication for studs and threaded rods with matching nuts and washers, mixed SKU orders, DIN / ISO / ASTM requirement review, and Hebei / Yongnian fastener cluster sourcing where applicable. Prices, MOQ, delivery lead time, packing details, and documentation are confirmed after manual RFQ review. Product images are representative only; final specifications should be confirmed by buyer drawings, standards, samples, or purchase lists.

Thread and Dimensional Considerations

Thread and dimension parameters determine whether a stud or threaded rod can meet the buyer's application requirements. Several parameters must be reviewed together.

  • Metric thread (ISO coarse or fine pitch)
  • UNC (Unified Coarse) thread
  • UNF (Unified Fine) thread
  • 8UN thread series where specified
  • Coarse and fine pitch
  • Right-hand and left-hand thread
  • Overall length
  • Threaded length at each end
  • Plain shank length for double-end studs
  • End chamfer
  • End point
  • Cut end
  • Burr removal
  • Thread tolerance or fit class
  • Nut fit confirmation
  • Coating allowance
  • Length tolerance
  • Straightness requirement
  • Cut-to-length requirements

Important: Overall length and threaded length are not the same parameter. Double-end studs require confirmed thread length at both ends separately. Tap-end studs may have different thread pitch or fit at each end. Metric and inch threads cannot be mixed. Coating thickness affects thread fit and must be reviewed with matching nuts. Cut-to-length threaded rods require end chamfer, burr removal and nut run-on confirmation. Diameter and overall length alone are not sufficient to define a stud or threaded rod.

Packing, Handling and Shipment Considerations

Long threaded rods, heavy stud bundles and mixed-length orders create packing and handling risks. These must be discussed in the RFQ stage rather than after production.

  • Rod length
  • Bundle weight
  • Carton weight
  • Wooden case or pallet requirement
  • End protection
  • Thread protection
  • Moisture protection
  • Rust prevention
  • Mixed length identification
  • SKU labels
  • Heat number or batch identification where required
  • Matching nuts packed together or separately
  • Container loading
  • Manual handling limits
  • Long cargo handling
  • Export packing

Important: Long threaded rods should not be packed in ordinary small cartons. Different lengths, grades, materials and finishes must be clearly separated. Thread end damage, bending and mix-up during transport must be avoided. Wooden cases, pallets, steel strapping and rust prevention requirements must be confirmed in the RFQ. Final packing method depends on length, weight, transport method and buyer requirements.

Common Procurement Mistakes

  • Calling every threaded product a "threaded rod".
  • Confusing stud bolts with fully threaded rods.
  • Confusing anchor rods with expansion anchors.
  • Quoting only diameter and length.
  • Forgetting thread pitch or TPI.
  • Mixing metric and inch threads.
  • Not specifying the thread length at each end.
  • Not defining the plain centre length.
  • Assuming both ends of a tap-end stud use the same thread.
  • Ignoring thread tolerance and nut fit.
  • Selecting a grade without confirming the applicable standard.
  • Assuming DIN, ISO and ASTM products are automatically interchangeable.
  • Forgetting matching nut grade and finish.
  • Ignoring coating effects on thread fit.
  • Not defining cut-end, chamfer or burr requirements.
  • Ignoring straightness on long threaded rods.
  • Using ordinary cartons for long or heavy rods without confirming handling.
  • Mixing different grades or lengths in one unclear RFQ line.
  • Approving production only from a representative image.
  • Requesting MTC or inspection documents only after production.

Product-Level FAQ

Short answers for buyers preparing technical studs and threaded rods RFQs.

What is the difference between a stud bolt and a threaded rod?

Stud bolts are typically used for flange and pressure equipment connections with defined thread lengths, materials, and grades per standards like ASTM A193 or DIN 976. Threaded rods are generally fully threaded along the entire length and are used for support, hanger, and general fastening applications. They are determined by the buyer's specification, not by appearance.

What is the difference between double-end studs and tap-end studs?

Double-end studs have threaded portions at both ends with a plain shank in the middle. Tap-end studs have one end designed to be screwed into a tapped hole while the other end receives a nut. Tap-end studs may have different thread pitches or lengths at each end and must be specified accordingly.

What information should buyers provide for a threaded rod RFQ?

Buyers should provide product type, standard or drawing, diameter, thread pitch or TPI, overall length, threaded length, material, grade, surface finish, matching nut requirements, quantity, packing, and inspection document requirements.

How should stud bolt length be measured?

Stud bolt length measurement depends on the standard. DIN and ISO studs may measure overall length differently from ASTM designs. The measuring method should be confirmed against the applicable standard or buyer drawing.

Why must buyers specify thread pitch or TPI?

Without thread pitch or TPI, the thread form cannot be determined. Different thread pitches affect nut fit, load distribution and application suitability. Diameter alone is insufficient.

Can metric and inch threaded products be substituted?

No. Metric threads (millimetre pitch) and inch threads (TPI) are different thread systems and cannot be substituted without changing the mating nut and application design.

Can DIN, ISO and ASTM studs be treated as interchangeable?

No. DIN, ISO and ASTM standards define different dimensional, material and property requirements. Products manufactured to different standards are not interchangeable unless specifically validated by the buyer's project specification.

What grades are commonly requested for stud bolts?

Common grade references include ASTM A193 B7, B8, B8M, ASTM A320 L7, L7M, ASTM A307 Grade A or Grade B, and ISO property classes such as 8.8 and 10.9. The applicable grade depends on the standard, material, service temperature, and project specification.

Why must matching nut grade and thread fit be confirmed?

Thread fit between studs and nuts must be compatible in pitch, tolerance, and coating allowance. Nut grade must match the stud grade and standard to ensure proper assembly and load performance.

How does hot-dip galvanizing affect threaded rods and nuts?

Hot-dip galvanizing adds coating thickness that affects thread fit. Nuts are typically tapped oversize after galvanising to accommodate the coating. Thread fit, nut grade and coating thickness must be confirmed together before production.

What packing is suitable for long threaded rods?

Long threaded rods should not be packed in ordinary small cartons. Wooden cases, steel strapping, end protectors, moisture protection and bundle weight limits must be confirmed in the RFQ. Packing method depends on length, weight, and transport method.

Can stud bolts and threaded rods be included in one mixed order?

Yes. Mixed orders can be reviewed manually when SKU data, diameters, lengths, grades, materials, finishes, quantities, packing, and inspection requirements are clear for each product type.

What documents can be discussed before shipment?

Depending on the item and buyer requirements, documents may include dimensional inspection reports, material test certificates (MTC), coating thickness reports, thread gauge checks, packing lists, and pre-shipment photos.

What affects the lead time for a mixed studs and threaded rods order?

Lead time depends on material availability, processing complexity (threading, heat treatment, coating), quantity, packing requirements, and inspection requirements. Final lead time is confirmed after RFQ review.
