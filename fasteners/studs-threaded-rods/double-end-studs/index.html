Direct Answer for Buyers

Buyers cannot specify only "double-end stud", a single overall length, or a product photo, because double-end studs differ from the following products:

  • Tap-end studs — may have different thread lengths, pitches or fits at each end for tapped-hole vs nut-side engagement
  • Stud bolts — typically fully threaded or with threaded ends without a prominent plain center section
  • Fully threaded rods — continuous threads along the entire length; no plain center section
  • Anchor rods — used with adhesive or cast-in-place for structural anchoring
  • Headed bolts — have a formed head at one end; different tightening mechanism

Double-end studs have threaded ends at both sides and may have a plain center shank. They cannot be specified by overall length and diameter alone. The buyer must also confirm thread lengths at both ends, plain center length, pitch, tolerance, and end form. Metric and inch threads cannot be mixed on the same stud unless explicitly specified. Coating thickness may affect thread fit and must be reviewed together with nut or tapped-hole requirements. No fixed strength, load capacity, torque, temperature or pressure data should be assumed without project-specific documentation.

Thread and Dimensional Considerations

Thread and dimensional specifications are the most critical parameters for double-end studs. The following parameters must be reviewed together with the applicable drawing, standard or BOM.

  • Metric or inch thread system
  • Diameter
  • Thread pitch (metric) or TPI (inch)
  • Overall length
  • Thread length at end A
  • Thread length at end B
  • Plain center length (if applicable)
  • Same or different thread at both ends
  • Thread tolerance class
  • Right-hand or left-hand thread
  • End chamfer or point
  • Full thread or partial thread at each end
  • Thread runout allowance
  • Undercut or relief groove
  • Concentricity
  • Straightness
  • Length tolerance
  • Squareness of ends

Key considerations:

  1. Two studs with the same overall length and diameter may be non-interchangeable due to different thread lengths, pitches, tolerances or end configurations.
  2. Metric and inch threads cannot be mixed on the same stud unless explicitly specified by the buyer's drawing or project specification.
  3. Thread length at each end must be confirmed independently.
  4. End chamfer or point should be specified per the applicable standard or drawing.
  5. Length, thread and tolerance must not be confirmed from a product photo alone.

Matching Nut, Tapped Hole and Assembly Considerations

Double-end studs engage with nuts at one or both ends, or with a tapped hole at one end. The nut and tapped-hole specifications must be compatible with the stud thread configuration.

  • Nut standard and grade
  • Nut thread size and pitch
  • Nut material (matching or superior to stud)
  • Nut finish (matching or compatible with stud)
  • Washer type and dimensions
  • Tapped-hole thread specification
  • Tapped-hole material
  • Tapped-hole depth
  • Tapped-hole thread tolerance
  • Assembly torque requirement
  • Lubrication requirement
  • Preload or tension requirement
  • Locking or prevailing torque requirement
  • Nut and stud marking requirement
  • Custom nut per buyer drawing

Key considerations:

  1. Nut material and strength must be compatible with the stud property class.
  2. Coating on the stud may require oversize tapping or upstream nut thread allowance.
  3. Tapped-hole engagement depends on thread length, thread tolerance and hole depth.
  4. Assembly torque and preload should be confirmed per engineering documentation.
  5. No fixed torque, preload or tension value should be assumed without project-specific documentation.

  • Thread protector type (plastic caps, metal caps, spiral protectors)
  • Thread protector at end A
  • Thread protector at end B
  • Packing quantity
  • Bag or box requirement
  • Bundle or bulk packing
  • Layer separation within carton
  • Carton weight
  • Pallet requirement
  • Strapping and wrapping
  • Label requirement
  • Batch traceability marking
  • Export packing per buyer instruction
  • Custom packing per drawing or BOM

Key considerations:

  1. Thread protectors at both ends should be specified for threaded sections.
  2. Plain center sections may require additional protection if surface finish is critical.
  3. Packing method depends on destination, transport mode and buyer requirements.

Common Procurement Mistakes

The following mistakes are commonly encountered in double-end stud procurement. Reviewing them before submitting an RFQ can reduce specification errors and quotation delays.

  1. Specifying only the overall length and diameter without confirming thread lengths at both ends.
  2. Confusing double-end studs with tap-end studs that have different thread configurations at each end.
  3. Confusing double-end studs with stud bolts that lack a prominent plain center section.
  4. Confusing double-end studs with fully threaded rods.
  5. Confusing double-end studs with anchor rods used for structural anchoring.
  6. Confusing double-end studs with headed bolts that have a formed head.
  7. Assuming equal thread lengths at both ends without confirming.
  8. Not specifying whether the thread at both ends is the same or different.
  9. Not specifying the thread pitch or TPI.
  10. Not confirming the thread tolerance class.
  11. Assuming metric and inch threads are compatible.
  12. Mixing metric and inch threads on the same stud without specifying.
  13. Not specifying whether the stud requires a right-hand or left-hand thread.
  14. Forgetting to specify end chamfer or point form.
  15. Not confirming whether nuts are required or included.
  16. Selecting a finish without checking thread fit and nut compatibility.
  17. Assuming coating thickness does not affect thread fit.
  18. Specifying a material grade without confirming the property class.
  19. Not specifying heat treatment or hardness requirements.
  20. Assuming all carbon steel studs are high tensile.
  21. Ordering a full thread length when a plain center section is required.
  22. Not specifying thread protector or packing requirements.
  23. Approving production only from a representative image.
  24. Using a standard reference as a substitute for a complete drawing or BOM.
  25. Assuming different standards are automatically equivalent.
  26. Requesting inspection documentation only after production.
  27. Not confirming whether the stud end requires a specific chamfer or point.
  28. Failing to specify thread runout or undercut requirements.

Product-Level FAQ

What is the difference between a double-end stud and a tap-end stud?

A double-end stud typically has threaded ends at both sides with a possible plain center section. A tap-end stud may have different thread lengths, pitches or fits at each end, where one end (the tap end) is designed for a tapped hole and the other end for a nut. They are not interchangeable. Buyers should confirm the specific stud type, thread configuration and end application per their drawing or BOM.

What is the difference between a double-end stud and a stud bolt?

A double-end stud has threaded ends with a plain center section. A stud bolt is typically fully threaded or has threaded ends without a prominent plain center. The dimensional configuration depends on the applicable standard or buyer drawing. Buyers should confirm the specific product type per their RFQ.

What is the difference between a double-end stud and a fully threaded rod?

A double-end stud has threaded sections at the ends and may have a plain center shank. A fully threaded rod has continuous threads along its entire length. They serve different applications and are not interchangeable. Buyers should confirm the product type per their drawing or standard.

What is the difference between a double-end stud and an anchor rod?

A double-end stud is a general-purpose stud with threaded ends for nut or tapped-hole engagement. Anchor rods are typically used with adhesive or cast-in-place for structural anchoring applications. They serve different applications and are not interchangeable.

What is the difference between a double-end stud and a headed bolt?

A double-end stud has no head; it has threaded ends at both sides. A headed bolt has a formed head at one end and a threaded section at the other. They use different tightening mechanisms and are not interchangeable. Buyers should confirm the product type per their drawing or application.

Can a double-end stud be specified by overall length and diameter alone?

No. A double-end stud cannot be specified by overall length and diameter alone. The buyer must also specify thread length at end A, thread length at end B, plain center length, thread pitch or TPI, thread tolerance, same or different thread at both ends, end chamfer, and applicable standard or drawing. Two studs with the same overall length and diameter may have different thread configurations and are not interchangeable.

What thread configurations are available for double-end studs?

Double-end studs may have same threads at both ends, different threads at each end, same or different thread pitches, right-hand or left-hand threads, and various thread tolerances. The thread configuration must be specified per the buyer's drawing, BOM or applicable standard.

What information should buyers provide for a double-end stud RFQ?

Buyers should provide product type, standard or drawing, metric or inch thread, diameter, thread pitch or TPI, overall length, thread length at end A, thread length at end B, plain center length, same or different thread at both ends, thread tolerance, right-hand or left-hand thread, end chamfer, material, property class or grade, surface finish, matching nut requirements, tapped-hole requirements, quantity by SKU, packing, thread protection, inspection documents and destination.

Can metric and inch threads be mixed on the same stud?

Metric and inch threads should not be mixed on the same stud unless explicitly specified by the buyer's drawing or project specification. Any mixed-thread configuration must be confirmed during manual RFQ review.

Does coating affect thread fit for double-end studs?

Yes. Coating thickness may affect thread fit, pitch diameter and assembly interference. Coating requirements must be reviewed together with the thread tolerance, matching nut or tapped-hole requirements. The final specification should be confirmed according to the buyer's drawing, BOM, or applicable standard.

What materials and property classes are commonly used?

Common materials include carbon steel in property classes 4.8, 5.8, 8.8, 10.9 and 12.9, and stainless steel in A2-70, A2-80, A4-70 and A4-80. Alloy steel and non-standard materials are also possible per buyer specification. The material and property class should be confirmed per the buyer's drawing, BOM, or applicable standard.

Are all double-end studs supplied with nuts?

No. Double-end studs may be ordered with or without matching nuts depending on the application and buyer requirements. If nuts are required, the nut standard, grade, material, finish and thread configuration should be specified in the RFQ.

What surface finishes are available for double-end studs?

Common finishes include plain (oiled), zinc plated, hot-dip galvanized, phosphate (manganese or zinc), Dacromet, Geomet and other coatings per buyer specification. The finish selection depends on the service environment, thread tolerance and nut compatibility. No fixed corrosion resistance rating should be assumed without project-specific confirmation.

Can double-end studs be included in a mixed fastener order?

Yes. Mixed orders can be reviewed manually when stud type, standard or drawing, thread configuration, dimensions, material, grade, finish, quantity, packing and inspection requirements are clear for each SKU.

What inspection documents can be discussed?

Depending on the item and buyer requirements, documents may include dimensional reports, material test certificates, mechanical property test reports, coating thickness reports, magnetic particle or dye penetrant inspection reports where applicable, packing lists and pre-shipment photos.

What affects the lead time for a double-end stud order?

Lead time depends on material availability, diameter, overall length, thread configuration, property class, heat treatment if applicable, coating, quantity, packing requirements, required documentation and inspection requirements. Final lead time is confirmed after RFQ review.

Can double-end studs be made to customer drawing?

Yes. Double-end studs can be made to the buyer's drawing, including non-standard dimensions, thread configurations, materials, finishes and tolerances. The drawing or BOM should define all critical dimensions, thread specifications and acceptance criteria.
