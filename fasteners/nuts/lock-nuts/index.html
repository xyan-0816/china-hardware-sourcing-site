Prevailing Torque Lock Nuts, Nylon Insert Lock Nuts and All-Metal Lock Nuts

Lock nuts from China are commonly sourced for applications where a standard nut is not sufficient to resist loosening under vibration, dynamic loading or other service conditions where nut loosening is a known concern. Common types include nylon insert prevailing torque nuts, all-metal prevailing torque nuts, prevailing torque flange nuts, serrated flange nuts, thin lock nuts and nuts made to drawing or project specification. Buyers should confirm lock nut type, applicable standard, lock nut metric or inch thread, thread pitch, material, property class, locking method, insert requirement, temperature and chemical exposure, mating bolt grade and finish, and reuse requirement before quotation. Standard references may include ISO 7040 for regular nylon insert nuts, ISO 7042 for all-metal high nuts, ISO 7719 for all-metal regular nuts, ISO 10511 for thin nylon insert nuts, ASME B18.16.6 for inch prevailing torque locknuts, ASME B18.16M for metric prevailing torque nuts, and project-specific drawings. We support sourcing communication for prevailing torque lock nuts, mixed SKU fastener orders, lock nut specification review, and Hebei / Yongnian fastener cluster sourcing. Prices, MOQ, delivery lead time, packing details, and inspection documents are confirmed after manual RFQ review. Direct Answer for Buyers

Buyers cannot specify only "lock nut", "nyloc nut" or "self-locking nut" because the following products are not the same:

  • Nylon insert prevailing torque nuts
  • All-metal prevailing torque nuts
  • Top-lock or distorted-thread nuts
  • Prevailing torque flange nuts
  • Serrated flange nuts
  • Thin lock nuts
  • Slotted or castellated nuts used with a cotter pin
  • Jam nuts used as part of a double-nut arrangement

Jam nuts, castellated nuts and serrated flange nuts use different locking principles from prevailing-torque lock nuts. They should not be treated as interchangeable solely because they are all sometimes called "lock nuts."

Functional Performance and Application Considerations

Lock nut performance depends on multiple factors. The following parameters should be reviewed together with the applicable standard and buyer requirements.

  • Prevailing torque at first installation
  • Prevailing torque after specified removal cycles
  • Removal torque
  • Reuse requirement
  • Number of reuse cycles if specified
  • Proof load or nut mechanical requirements
  • Thread fit
  • Bolt surface condition
  • Nut surface condition
  • Lubrication
  • Temperature exposure
  • Chemical exposure
  • Vibration
  • Dynamic loading
  • Installation speed
  • Installation tool
  • Tightening method
  • Clamp force requirement
  • Inspection or test method

Important considerations:

  1. Prevailing torque is not the same as final tightening torque.
  2. Locking performance cannot be determined from the colour of a nylon insert alone.
  3. Non-metallic insert suitability must be reviewed together with temperature and chemical exposure conditions.
  4. All-metal lock nuts are not automatically suitable for every high-temperature environment.
  5. Reuse capability must be confirmed according to the applicable standard, test requirements and buyer documentation.
  6. No fixed torque value, fixed temperature range or fixed reuse count can be provided without project-specific information.
  7. Website content cannot substitute for the buyer's engineering verification and assembly testing.

Common Procurement Mistakes

  • Calling every anti-loosening nut a "lock nut".
  • Treating nylon insert nuts and all-metal lock nuts as interchangeable.
  • Identifying a lock nut only from a photograph.
  • Quoting only thread diameter.
  • Forgetting thread pitch or TPI.
  • Mixing metric and inch threads.
  • Mixing coarse and fine threads.
  • Ignoring regular, high and thin nut styles.
  • Assuming all nylon inserts have the same material and temperature capability.
  • Selecting products only by insert colour.
  • Assuming all-metal lock nuts are suitable for every high-temperature application.
  • Ignoring bolt grade and nut property class compatibility.
  • Ignoring coating effects on thread fit and prevailing torque.
  • Assuming every lock nut can be reused repeatedly.
  • Confusing prevailing torque with final tightening torque.
  • Treating serrated flange nuts as identical to prevailing-torque nuts.
  • Treating castellated nuts as prevailing-torque nuts.
  • Using thin jam nuts as a direct substitute for engineered locking nuts.
  • Approving production only from a representative image.
  • Requesting performance tests or inspection documents only after production.

Product-Level FAQ

Short answers for buyers preparing lock nut RFQs.

What is a prevailing torque lock nut?

A prevailing torque lock nut is a nut designed to resist loosening through friction generated by a locking feature. The locking feature may be a non-metallic insert or an all-metal deformed thread section. The prevailing torque is the friction torque present before the nut contacts the bearing surface.

What is the difference between a nylon insert lock nut and an all-metal lock nut?

A nylon insert lock nut uses a non-metallic collar at the top to create prevailing torque friction. An all-metal lock nut relies on controlled thread deformation or a mechanical locking feature in the metal nut body. They are not interchangeable. Selection depends on the applicable standard, temperature, chemical exposure, and reuse requirements.

What is the difference between a regular, high and thin lock nut?

Regular, high and thin lock nuts have different nut heights. A regular nut has a standard height range. A high nut has increased height for greater thread engagement. A thin nut has reduced height and is used where space is limited. They are not interchangeable for the same application.

What information should buyers provide for a lock nut RFQ?

Buyers should provide lock nut type, locking method, applicable standard, metric or inch thread, diameter, thread pitch or TPI, regular or high or thin style, material, property class, insert type if applicable, surface finish, mating bolt standard and grade, temperature and chemical exposure, reuse requirements, quantity, packing and inspection documents.

Can nylon insert lock nuts be used at high temperatures?

Nylon inserts have temperature limitations. The specific material, application temperature and performance requirements must be confirmed. All-metal lock nuts may be considered for higher temperature applications, but suitability depends on the exact grade, standard and application conditions.

Are all-metal lock nuts suitable for every high-temperature application?

No. All-metal lock nuts are not automatically suitable for every high-temperature application. The exact material, locking design, applicable standard and service conditions must be confirmed. General claims about high-temperature capability cannot substitute for project-specific verification.

Can lock nuts be reused?

Reuse depends on the nut type, applicable standard, and the buyer's specific reuse requirements. Not all lock nuts are designed for reuse. Prevailing torque performance after repeated use should be confirmed against the applicable test standard.

What is the difference between prevailing torque and installation torque?

Prevailing torque is the friction torque generated by the locking feature before the nut contacts the bearing surface. Installation torque (tightening torque) is the total torque applied to achieve clamp force, which includes prevailing torque plus bearing surface friction. They are not the same parameter.

Why must the mating bolt grade and finish be confirmed?

Lock nut performance depends on the mating bolt. The bolt grade, surface finish, coating, thread tolerance and hardness all affect the prevailing torque and locking behaviour. A lock nut tested on one bolt condition may not perform identically on another.

Can metric and inch lock nuts be substituted?

No. Metric and inch thread systems are different. Thread diameter, pitch and thread form are not interchangeable.

Can coarse-thread and fine-thread lock nuts be substituted?

No. Coarse and fine threads have different pitches, depths and strength characteristics. They are not interchangeable.

Are DIN 985 and ISO 7040 automatically identical?

DIN 985 and ISO 7040 are related but may have differences in dimensions, material requirements and performance specifications. Buyers should confirm the applicable standard, version, dimensions and requirements for their specific product.

What is the difference between a serrated flange nut and a prevailing-torque flange nut?

A serrated flange nut has serrations on the flange bearing surface designed to resist loosening through mechanical engagement with the mating surface. A prevailing-torque flange nut has a prevailing torque locking feature in the nut body. They use different locking principles and are not automatically interchangeable.

Is a castellated nut a prevailing-torque lock nut?

No. A castellated nut achieves positive locking through a cotter pin or other mechanical locking device inserted through slots in the nut. It is not a prevailing-torque lock nut. The locking principle and application requirements are different.

Can nylon insert and all-metal lock nuts be included in one mixed order?

Yes. Mixed orders can be reviewed manually when type, locking method, thread size, style, material, grade, finish, quantity, packing and inspection requirements are clear for each product type.

What inspection documents or performance tests can be discussed?

Depending on the item and buyer requirements, documents may include dimensional reports, material test certificates, coating thickness reports, prevailing torque test reports, proof load test reports, and pre-shipment photos.

What affects the lead time for a mixed lock nut order?

Lead time depends on material availability, nut type, locking element type, heat treatment if applicable, coating, quantity, packing requirements and inspection requirements. Final lead time is confirmed after RFQ review.
