Direct Answer for Buyers

Buyers cannot specify only "machine screw", a single diameter, or a product photo, because machine screws differ from the following products:

  • Bolts — typically used with nuts in through-hole assemblies, generally available in larger diameters
  • Self-tapping screws — form or cut their own thread in the mating material, do not require pre-tapped holes
  • Self-drilling screws — have a drilling point that creates a pilot hole during installation
  • Thread-forming screws — form their own thread in an unthreaded hole by displacing material

Machine screws typically use a machine thread designed for pre-tapped holes or matching nuts. They do not form or cut their own thread. The final specification depends on the buyer's drawing, applicable standard and application conditions.

Thread and Dimension Considerations

Thread and dimensional parameters affect fit, assembly performance and compatibility with mating components. The following should be reviewed together with the applicable standard and buyer requirements.

  • Metric or inch thread
  • Diameter
  • Pitch (metric) or TPI (inch)
  • Length
  • Thread length (full thread or partial thread)
  • Head style
  • Head diameter and height
  • Drive type (slotted, Phillips, Pozidriv, Torx, hex socket, hex head)
  • Thread tolerance
  • Point type
  • Countersink requirement (for flat head)
  • Shoulder diameter and length (for shoulder screws)
  • Surface condition
  • Dimensional tolerance
  • Drawing-controlled products

Key considerations:

  1. Metric and inch thread systems are not interchangeable.
  2. The same diameter may correspond to different pitches (coarse or fine). Pitch must be confirmed.
  3. Head style affects bearing surface, drive type and overall assembly dimensions.
  4. Full thread and partial thread serve different clamping and shear applications.
  5. Shoulder screws have a precision-machined shoulder that must be confirmed per drawing.
  6. Thread and dimensions must not be confirmed from a product photo alone.

Common Procurement Mistakes

The following mistakes are commonly encountered in machine screw procurement. Reviewing them before submitting an RFQ can reduce specification errors and quotation delays.

  1. Calling every small threaded fastener a "machine screw" without confirming the product type.
  2. Confusing machine screws with self-tapping screws — machine screws require pre-tapped holes or nuts.
  3. Confusing machine screws with self-drilling screws — self-drilling screws create their own pilot hole.
  4. Confusing machine screws with thread-forming screws — thread-forming screws form their own thread.
  5. Specifying only the diameter without confirming the pitch or TPI.
  6. Forgetting to specify the head style.
  7. Forgetting to specify the drive type.
  8. Forgetting to specify the thread length (full or partial thread).
  9. Assuming metric and inch machine screws are interchangeable.
  10. Assuming DIN and ISO references are automatically identical.
  11. Treating shoulder screws as standard machine screws.
  12. Not confirming the matching nut or threaded hole standard.
  13. Selecting a coating without checking compatibility with mating components.
  14. Assuming black oxide provides high outdoor corrosion resistance.
  15. Mixing different head styles or sizes in one unclear RFQ line.
  16. Approving production only from a representative image.
  17. Requesting inspection documents only after production.
  18. Failing to define export packing and label requirements.

Product-Level FAQ

What is the difference between a machine screw and a bolt?

Machine screws are typically used in pre-tapped holes or with matching nuts, and are generally available in smaller diameters. Bolts are typically used with nuts in through-hole assemblies and are generally available in larger diameters. The distinction depends on the applicable standard, head form, thread type and application. Buyers should confirm the product type per their drawing or specification.

What is the difference between a machine screw and a self-tapping screw?

A machine screw has a machine thread designed for use in a pre-tapped hole or with a matching nut. A self-tapping screw forms or cuts its own thread in the mating material. They are not interchangeable. Machine screws require a threaded mating component; self-tapping screws do not.

What is the difference between a machine screw and a self-drilling screw?

A machine screw has a standard machine thread for use in pre-tapped holes or with nuts. A self-drilling screw has a drilling point that creates its own pilot hole during installation. They serve different assembly functions and are not interchangeable.

What is the difference between a machine screw and a thread-forming screw?

A machine screw is designed for use in a pre-tapped hole or with a matching nut. A thread-forming screw forms its own thread in an unthreaded hole by displacing material. They use different thread designs and are not interchangeable.

What information should buyers provide for a machine screw RFQ?

Buyers should provide head style, drive type, applicable standard or drawing, metric or inch thread, diameter, pitch or TPI, length, thread length, material, property class, surface finish, matching nut or threaded hole standard, application, quantity by SKU, packing, inspection documents and destination.

What head styles are commonly available for machine screws?

Common head styles include pan head, flat head, round head, socket head, button head, cheese head and shoulder. Each head style has different dimensions, bearing surface and drive type. Buyers should confirm the specific head style per the applicable standard or drawing.

Can machine screws be used without a pre-tapped hole?

No. Machine screws require a pre-tapped hole or a matching nut. They do not form or cut their own thread. If the mating material is unthreaded, a self-tapping or thread-forming screw may be required instead.

Can metric and inch machine screws be substituted?

No. Metric and inch thread systems have different diameters, pitches and thread forms. They are not interchangeable. Buyers should confirm the thread system per their application requirements.

Can DIN and ISO machine screw references be treated as automatically identical?

No. DIN and ISO references are related but may have differences in dimensions, tolerances, head forms and material requirements. Buyers should confirm the applicable standard, version and requirements for their specific product.

What is the difference between full thread and partial thread machine screws?

A full thread machine screw has threads along the entire shank. A partial thread machine screw has an unthreaded shank section. The choice depends on the application, clamping force and design requirements. Buyers should confirm the thread length per the applicable standard or drawing.

What drive types are commonly available for machine screws?

Common drive types include slotted, Phillips, Pozidriv, Torx, hex socket and hex head. The drive type affects installation torque, tool compatibility and head appearance. Buyers should confirm the drive type per the applicable standard or drawing.

Can shoulder screws be treated as standard machine screws?

No. A shoulder screw has a precision-machined unthreaded shoulder section between the head and the threaded portion. It is used for alignment, pivoting or spacing applications. Standard machine screws do not have this shoulder feature. They are not interchangeable.

Can machine screws be included in a mixed fastener order?

Yes. Mixed orders can be reviewed manually when head style, drive type, standard, thread size, length, material, property class, finish, quantity, packing and inspection requirements are clear for each SKU.

What inspection documents can be discussed?

Depending on the item and buyer requirements, documents may include dimensional reports, material test certificates, coating thickness reports, packing lists and pre-shipment photos.

What affects the lead time for a mixed machine screw order?

Lead time depends on material availability, head style, drive type, standard, dimensions, heat treatment if applicable, coating, quantity, packing requirements and inspection requirements. Final lead time is confirmed after RFQ review.

Why must the matching nut or threaded hole standard be confirmed for machine screws?

Machine screws require a matching threaded component. The nut or threaded hole standard, thread class, material and strength must be compatible with the machine screw. Mismatched threads may cause assembly failure or poor performance.
