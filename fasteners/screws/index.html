Buyer Quick Answers

Short answers for commonly asked screw sourcing questions.

1. What are industrial screws?

Industrial screws are threaded fasteners designed for a range of assembly applications including machinery, construction, metalworking, woodworking, and manufactured products. They are specified by type, standard, thread, head, drive, point, material, finish, and dimensional requirements.

2. Machine screws vs self-tapping screws: what is the difference?

Machine screws are designed for use with tapped holes or mating nuts and have uniform machine threads. Self-tapping screws form or cut their own mating threads in a prepared substrate and may require a pilot hole. The selection depends on the substrate, standard, and assembly requirements.

3. Self-tapping screws vs self-drilling screws: what is the difference?

Self-tapping screws form or cut mating threads and commonly require a prepared pilot hole. Self-drilling screws include a drill point intended to create a hole before thread engagement. Selection depends on substrate material, thickness, point design, and installation conditions.

4. What are socket head cap screws used for?

Socket head cap screws are commonly used in machinery, tooling, and equipment assemblies where a recessed hex drive is required. They are typically specified by standard (e.g. DIN 912 or ISO 4762), diameter, length, material, and property class.

5. Metric screws vs inch screws: can they be interchangeable?

No. Metric and inch screws are not automatically interchangeable. The thread pitch or TPI, diameter, and dimensional system differ between the two. Buyers should confirm the original specification. See the Metric and Inch Fastener Conversion Guide for reference.

6. How do screw thread types affect selection?

Screw thread type affects the assembly method and application. Machine threads require tapped holes or nuts, self-tapping threads form mating threads in a substrate, and self-drilling points both drill and tap in one step. Buyers should confirm thread type by the applicable standard, substrate material, and installation method.

7. Phillips, hex socket and Torx drive types: how to choose?

The drive type should be selected based on installation tooling, torque requirements, and cam-out risk. Phillips drives are common for general assembly. Hex socket drives are typical for machinery. Torx drives offer increased torque transfer and reduced cam-out. The appropriate drive should be confirmed by the applicable standard or drawing.

8. Carbon steel screws vs stainless steel screws: what is the difference?

Carbon steel screws are commonly selected for general-purpose fastening but require a surface finish for corrosion protection. Stainless steel screws are typically chosen where corrosion resistance is needed. Buyers should confirm the exact material grade (e.g. A2/304 or A4/316) and whether a plated or passivated finish is required.

9. Zinc plated vs other surface finishes for screws: what should buyers know?

Zinc plating provides a common level of corrosion protection for indoor or light outdoor applications. Black oxide and black phosphate are typically used for appearance or oil-holding purposes. Nickel plating offers a different appearance. The finish should be reviewed with the screw type, application environment, and project specification.

10. What information is required for a screw RFQ?

A complete screw RFQ should include screw type, standard or drawing, diameter, length, thread type, head type, drive type, point type, material, mechanical requirement, surface finish, quantity, packing requirements, and inspection document requirements.

11. Can screws be included in mixed fastener orders?

Yes. Screws can be included in mixed fastener orders when each SKU has a clear screw type, standard or drawing, diameter, length, thread, head, drive, point, material, finish, quantity, and packing requirement. Mixed orders should list screws separately from bolts, nuts, washers, and other fasteners.

Screws FAQ

What information is required for a screw RFQ?

Provide the screw type, applicable standard or drawing, diameter, length, thread, head, drive, point, material, mechanical requirement, finish, quantity, packing, documentation, and destination. Application and substrate details are also useful for review.

Can screws be supplied according to drawings?

Custom drawing screws can be reviewed when the drawing clearly defines dimensions, tolerances, material, mechanical properties, finish, inspection criteria, and order quantity. Feasibility is confirmed during manual RFQ review.

Which materials and finishes are commonly available?

Common directions include carbon steel, stainless steel 304 or 316, alloy steel, and brass, with zinc plating, black oxide, black phosphate, nickel plating, passivation, or project-specified coatings. Availability and suitability depend on the exact screw specification.

Can head and drive combinations be customized?

Head and drive combinations may be reviewed for standard or drawing-based screws. Availability depends on screw type, diameter, length, tooling, production standard, quantity, and mechanical requirements.

What inspection documents can be discussed during RFQ review?

Depending on the item and buyer requirements, dimensional reports, material certificates, coating thickness reports, hardness tests, thread checks, drilling or tapping performance records, packing lists, and pre-shipment inspection evidence may be discussed.
